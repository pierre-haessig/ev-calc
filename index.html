Electric vs Thermal vehicle calculator

About this calculator

This app is a work in progress. Please report any issue on GitHub or by email.

With this interactive calculator, you can compare the ecological merit (in terms of lifecycle CO2 emissions) of an electric vehicle (EV) with a thermal vehicle motored by an internal combustion engine (ICE).

The general idea is that, for its manufacturing, an EV requires more energy than an ICE car because of its battery. The EV has thus a “CO2 debt” that can be repaid after traveling some distance if, as one may expect, the EV indeed emits less CO2 during usage (CO2 per unit distance). The computation of this “distance to CO2 parity” is the objective of this calculator.

Distance to CO2 parity depends on several inputs which value is difficult to know precisely, so this calculator allows you to input lower and upper bounds in addition to nominal values. Then, it propagates this uncertainty intervals down to the final result. Playing a bit with the numbers, in particular using coal-based electricity for EV charging, the distance can get negative (EV loses to ICE)! Also, wide input uncertainty can lead to infinite uncertainty for parity distance.

My conclusion on this is clear: all vehicles should be light and small, and, if electric, should be recharged with green electricity. Indeed, with a small battery, EVs are definetely better than combustion-powered cars. My e-bike has only a 300 Wh (0.3 kWh) battery and consumes 6—11 Wh/km.

How much energy does it takes to manufacture 1 kWh of battery, including all processes like mining raw materials? And how much greenhouse gases does it emits?

Battery manufacturing impact is the weak point of EVs, creating a huge “emissions debt” before their first drive. Still, manufacturing is getting more and more efficient (as well as cost effective). In the 2010-2020 decade, the change is rapid, so that impact figures becomes rapidly outdated!

The heavier the car, the higher fuel consumption!

Also, one should preferably use real driving consumption figures, rather than standardized test results which are over optimistic (especially the European NEDC test which is now, at last, replaced by WLTP).

Diesel engines are genelly more efficient than gasoline engines, but one should not forget the 13% extra emissions per volume of this fuel! This reduces, but does not cancels, the advantage of Diesel.

Presets (fuel consumption of a compact car):

kgCO2/l,

Each fuel emits a given amount of CO2 per unit volume. Diesel emits about 13% more than Gasoline, mostly because of its higher density.

Most emissions happen at the vehicle exhaust (direct emissions), but one should not forget upstream emissions (“Well-to-Tank”) during the manufacturing (mostly: extraction of crude oil and refining). In the end, the "Well-to-Wheels" (WTW) emissions are about 1.2 higher than direct emissions.
